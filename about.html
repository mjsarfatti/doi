About

EU Integrity Watch is a user-friendly database that provides a unique overview of the activities of members of the European Parliament (MEPs) and enables a range of rankings and visual comparisons. It also allows to better monitor potential conflicts of interests or to identify those MEPs with a high degree of external activity.

EU Integrity Watch was launched in October 2014 by the Transparency International EU Office (TI-EU). The project is co-funded by the Open Society Initiative for Europe (OSIFE), with a contribution by the King Baudouin Foundation.

Background on lobbying and conflicts of interest

Over recent years, public policy debates have been giving increasing attention to the importance of safeguarding the integrity of parliamentarians and their activities.  As the scope of interaction between public and private sectors increases, for example through the greater privatisation of public services, the systematic use of external expertise to feed into law-making, and individuals moving in and out of each sector over the course of their careers, ethics regimes in public institutions need to be sufficient to deal with heightened integrity and corruption risks.  As the OECD noted in their 2005 Guidelines for Managing Conflict of Interest in the Public Service, without adequate identification and management of potential conflicts of interest, the opportunities for public figures to abuse their positions for personal gain increase.  Protections therefore need to be in place to ensure that legislators serve the public interest and not narrow, vested interests.

The situation in Europe is mixed.  In our 2012 report ‘Money, Politics, Power: Corruption Risks in Europe ’, Transparency International looked at the anti-corruption safeguards in 25 European countries.  Our research found that while only a small number of these countries had codes of conduct in place for parliamentarians, the majority do require legislators to declare their interests, assets and outside income.  However, problems were noticed in the verification mechanisms in many countries, affecting public confidence in the veracity of declarations.

In its 2014 EU Anti-Corruption Report, the European Commission identified similar concerns, highlighting the lack of checks on financial declarations of national MPs as a major weakness in the integrity systems of many EU countries. Verifications on the substance of declarations of interests were found often to be ‘formalistic and mostly limited to administrative checks’, with the tools and capacity to carry out substantial checks deemed to be ‘insufficient’.

Background on the European Parliament’s Code of Conduct

As a reaction to the cash-for-amendments scandal that hit the European Parliament in 2011 (in which four members accepted payments from undercover journalists posing as lobbyists in exchange for the introduction of legislative amendments) a new code of conduct was introduced in 2012.

The Code obliges Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) to submit information on their financial interests, to declare their attendance at third party events and to reveal any gifts that they have received.

On paper the 2012 Code of Conduct meant significant progress and the reporting obligations were quite extensive at the time. Unfortunately the implementation proved less ambitious. Despite recommendations by the Advisory Committee to the President of the European Parliament finding that seven MEPs had violated the Code of Conduct, not a single one has been sanctioned to date.

National Parliaments across Europe – Germany, France and the United Kingdom in particular – have significantly tightened their rules and reporting obligations during the last two years. Often these steps came in direct responses to scandals. In comparison, the European Parliament is now falling behind standards at national level, and this in a moment when euro-scepticism and public perception of the EU institutions as corrupt are at an all-time high.

In this context Transparency International has published a comprehensive review of the integrity of 10 EU Institutions, including the European Parliament, Commission Council and the European Court of Justice (EUIS report). Transparency International is using the findings to advocate change inside the institutions and has recommended a reform of the Code of Conduct of the European Parliament that would establish an independent ethics committee to monitor potential conflicts of interest.

Background on the EU Integrity Watch project

Following the elections to the European Parliament in May, the new Parliament took office on the 1st of July 2014. All members – new and old – had until the 3rd of July to submit their declarations of financial interest, thereby submitting their information on outside revenues, board memberships and financial holdings.

Policy recommendations

This user-friendly database provides a unique overview of the activities of the newly elected European Parliament and enables a range of rankings and visual comparisons. It also allows to better monitor potential conflicts of interest or to identify those MEPs with a high degree of external activity.

For that purpose Transparency International has developed an “External Activity Indicator (EAI)” (Methodology), which scores each MEP based on the number of outside occupations, board memberships or other political mandates as well as the cumulative income derived from these activities.

Transparency International policy recommendations

One conclusion that can already be drawn from the EU Integrity Watch database is that the declarations of financial interest and thus the Code of Conduct of the European Parliament need reform. Meaningful monitoring of conflicts of interest is impossible when MEPs declare that their side-job is “consultant”, “freelancer”, “manager” or something that goes under an abbreviation such as “RvC FMO” or “ASDCAM”. The financial thresholds in the declarations need to be revised and should allow much more accurate reporting instead of capping the information at “more than 10,000 EUR”. Better guidelines on how to fill out the declarations are needed and the European Parliament should better monitor the submitted declarations. An independent ethics committee should monitor compliance and publish recommendations in case of an alleged breach.

To further improve public monitoring of potential conflicts of interest at the Parliament and at the Commission, TI-EU suggests the adoption of a ‘legislative footprint’ mechanism – a record of all meetings between lobbyists and lawmakers. 181 MEPs have already committed to using this as part of TI-EU’s anti-corruption election pledge.

Finally, TI-EU continues to advocate for a mandatory Transparency Register of Lobbyists that covers the European Parliament, the Commission and the Council, something that Commission President elect Jean-Claude Juncker has announced for his term.

Core recommendations:

  • Publish more detailed information in the declarations to allow a meaningful monitoring of potential conflicts of interest

  • Revise the financial thresholds in the declaration to allow more accurate reporting and monitoring of outside revenue

  • Translate all declarations into all official languages

  • The European Parliament administration should better check the declarations for plausibility and possible errors – false declarations should lead to sanctions

  • The Code of Conduct needs to be fully respected in word and in spirit and violations need to be sanctioned by the EP President

  • Standardise as much of the information as possible to allow better comparisons and groupings and to connect the information with other databases, such as the Transparency Register

  • Publish all declarations of interest in a centralised, searchable manner, including historical declarations (.xml/.json files instead of .pdf)




 

Methodology

EU Integrity Watch was launched in October 2014 by the Transparency International EU Office (TI-EU). The project is co-funded by the Open Society Initiative for Europe (OSIFE).

Where does the data come from‌?

All data contained in the EU Integrity Watch database comes from the website of the European Parliament. EU Integrity Watch automatically extracts the information from the original declarations that are published in Pdf format on the Parliament's website and uses them to regularly update its own database. The date of the latest update is featured on our website. EU Integrity Watch is not responsible for the data that is provided by the European Parliament. All information contained in the original declaration is under the sole responsibility of the member of the European Parliament that filled out and signed the declaration. Given that the Parliament only provides the data in Pdf format instead of a reliable .xml format our data might not be 100% accurate. Please report any inaccuracies that you might spot to dfreund@transparency.org

How do Members of the European Parliament declare their financial interests‌?

The Code of Conduct of the European Parliament establishes that for reasons of transparency, Members of the European Parliament (MEPs) shall submit a declaration of financial interests. Those declarations shall then be published on Parliament's website. The declarations are the sole responsibility of each individual member. EU Integrity Watch merely copies the information contained in the declarations and makes it available on its website. This means that the information is currently only available in the language in which the member has filled out the declaration.

On each profile of a member we link to the profile on the Parliament's own website, where the original declaration and all other information can be accessed and verified.


Why are all revenues provided as a range‌?

According to the Code of Conduct of the European Parliament all outside revenues or financial interests shall be placed in one of the following categories:

  • 500 - 1,000 EUR per month

  • 1,001 - 5,000 EUR per month

  • 5,001 - 10,000 EUR per month

  • More than 10,000 EUR per month

In case of revenues of less than 500 EUR per month or 5,000 per year no category needs to be indicated.


What is the External Activity Indicator (EAI)‌?

The External Activity Indicator was developed by Transparency International to provide a quick overview of all activities that fall under the remits of the Code of Conduct and the declarations of financial interests. In short, the higher the indicator the more activities a member has declared and the higher the declared revenues. The website shades MEPs according to their EAI - the higher the EAI the darker the grey shading.

The External Activity Indicator is not an assessment of an MEP's performance or a judgement on the declared activities. It does not identify actual conflicts of interest. It only serves to quickly and easily identify those MEPs that have a higher number of declared activities or a higher declared income from outside activities.

Transparency International fully supports the European Parliament in its efforts towards greater transparency. Therefore we encourage all Members of the European Parliament to provide detailed and accurate declarations of their activities and financial interests. Only those declarations that provide meaningful and detailed information will allow the public and civil society to monitor potential conflicts of interests.


How is the External Activity Indicator (EAI) calculated‌?

The External Activity Indicator is calculated by an algorithm that allocates points for each activity declared in a Member's declaration of financial interest. If no activities are declared a member has an EAI of 0. Points are attributed as follows:

  • 1 point for each activity (2 points if the activity is a "regular remunerated activity")

  • 1 points for each financial holding

  • 1 point for every 500 EUR of minimum monthly outside revenue

  • 10 points if revenue of "more than 10,000 EUR per month" are declared in at least one activity
